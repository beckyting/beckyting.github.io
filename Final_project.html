From Rules to Leverage

The Weaponization of U.S. Foreign Economic Policy

By Becky Ting, Nathaniel Pendleton, Jennifer Gao, Ayush Dubey  ·  April 7, 2026

Executive Summary

The United States has long used economic power as a tool of statecraft. But something has changed. What was once episodic and purposeful has become routine and increasingly untethered from any defined strategic end.

The core argument: Economic weaponization is not new but today's version is different. The U.S. now deploys its structural chokepoints on the global economy as a default instrument of foreign policy, without a clear blueprint for the order it wants to build or the behavior it wants to change. This is not a strategy. Leverage for leverage's sake has consequences.

This analysis traces how we got here, maps the chokepoints at stake, surveys competing perspectives. Our policy recommendation is built on three pillars: Offense - using current American leverages to maximise U.S. benefits, Defense - defending U.S. economic vulnerabilities, and Credibility - restoring U.S. government's credibility with other nation states and the private sector. It also assumes that once a nation state starts weaponising its economic policies, it'll spread across other countries and is hard to contain.

Part I
Historical Foundation

Economic coercion didn't start with tariffs.

From Lend-Lease to the Suez Crisis to the Russia sanctions of 2022, the United States has wielded economic power against adversaries and allies alike, but each with a clear end goal.

Click each event to expand its strategic significance:

1941
Episodic Lend-Lease Act
The U.S. supplied Allied powers with war materiel in exchange for political alignment. Economic power was deployed with the goal of defeating the Axis and anchor a U.S.-led postwar order. The tool served the strategy.
1944
Systemic Bretton Woods & Dollar Primacy
The postwar financial architecture embedded the dollar as the global reserve currency, giving the U.S. structural leverage over every nation that traded in dollar-denominated markets. This was the foundation of the US chokepoint that still exists today.
1956
Episodic Suez Crisis
The U.S. threatened to withhold IMF support for a collapsing British pound unless the UK withdrew from the Suez Canal. Britain complied within days. Economic leverage deployed surgically with a defined target and a defined desired behavior change.
1971
Structural Shift Nixon Shock
Nixon unilaterally ended dollar-gold convertibility, demonstrating that the U.S. was willing to restructure the global financial system unilaterally when it served domestic interests, even at the cost of allies' interests.
1985
Episodic Plaza Accord
The U.S. coordinated with G5 partners to depreciate the dollar against the yen and deutschmark, correcting global trade imbalances through coordinated currency intervention. Coercion with multilateral buy-in — the model that defenders of re-institutionalization still invoke today.
2022
Structural Shift Russia Sanctions & Asset Freezes
Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, the U.S. and allies froze ~$300B in Russian central bank assets and expelled Russia from SWIFT. The scale and speed signaled that dollar-denominated assets were no longer safe from geopolitical risks, a message heard loudly in Beijing, Riyadh, and beyond.
2025–
Structural Shift Trump II: Weaponization Without Blueprint
Under Trump's second term, tariffs, sanctions, and export controls are deployed simultaneously across allies and adversaries alike, with competing visions inside the administration itself. Protectionism, dealmaking, hawkishness, and skepticism coexist without resolution. The tool has become the policy, not the instrument of a policy.
Part II
The Architecture of Leverage

The global economy runs through a handful of chokepoints. The U.S. controls most of them but not all.

Understanding who controls what economic chokepoints helps us understand how economonic policies can be weaponised.

Click any item to see how the chokepoint is weaponized in practice:

🇺🇸 U.S.-Controlled Chokepoints

💵
Dollar & SWIFT Payment Rails
~88% of global FX transactions involve dollars. SWIFT exclusion can functionally cut a country off from the international financial system. Weaponized against Iran (2012), Russia (2022), and threatened against others. The risk: overuse erodes the dollar's status as a safe asset.
⚖️
Legal-Jurisdictional Reach
U.S. courts and regulators can reach entities that touches the dollar system, including foreign banks. Secondary sanctions extend this jurisdiction further, threatening non-U.S. firms that do business with sanctioned parties. Functionally a global regulatory chokepoint.
🔬
Frontier Technology
Export controls on advanced semiconductors (the October 2022 and subsequent Commerce Dept. rules) restrict China's access to chips needed for AI and military applications. The U.S. controls this chokepoint through EDA software, chip design IP, and equipment made by ASML (Netherlands) and Applied Materials — though allies must remain coordinated.
🏪
U.S. Market Access
Access to the world's largest consumer market ($27T GDP) is used as leverage through tariffs, import quotas, and WTO threats. Tariffs are the most visible but, as Fishman argues, the weakest form of leverage: they are economically costly, politically visible, and easily routed around.
🛡️
Security Umbrella
U.S. military presence and alliance commitments, NATO, the Pacific alliances, generate diplomatic leverage. Allies pay a form of geopolitical rent for this protection, which creates leverage in trade and technology negotiations. Risk: if credibility erodes, so does this chokepoint.

⚠️ Foreign Chokepoints / U.S. Vulnerabilities

⛏️
Critical Minerals Ecosystem
China controls not just mining but the full value chain: processing, magnet production, licensing, and downstream manufacturing. This is an ecosystem chokepoint that cannot be quickly replicated. Rare earths, lithium processing, and permanent magnets are the acute vulnerabilities.
🏭
Manufacturing Dominance
China produces the majority of the world's batteries, EVs, solar panels, and active pharmaceutical ingredients. Disruption would cascade across industries from defense to healthcare. Unlike frontier chips, these are commodity-scale dependencies.
🇹🇼
Taiwan Semiconductor Concentration
TSMC produces the majority of the world's leading-edge chips. A Taiwan contingency — blockade, conflict, or coercive pressure — would disrupt global semiconductor supply in ways the CHIPS Act alone cannot quickly remedy.
💊
Pharmaceutical Ingredients
China and India supply the majority of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) and precursor chemicals. U.S. dependence on Chinese API supply is particularly acute for generic drugs, antibiotics, and some biologics, a vulnerability that became visible during COVID-19.
🌊
Undersea Cables
95%+ of global internet traffic travels through undersea cables vulnerable to sabotage and hard to defense.
💵
Treasury Holdings
Foreign ownership of U.S. Treasuries creates a potential financial pressure point, though large-scale dumping would hurt the other nations too. These are asymmetric vulnerabilities: cheap to threaten, expensive to defend.

Part III
The Present Moment

The tools are the same. The strategy is conflicting.

Under Trump's second term, the full arsenal — tariffs, export controls, sanctions, CFIUS reviews, market access threats — is being deployed simultaneously, against allies and adversaries alike, by an administration with four competing internal visions and no unified doctrine.

Four Competing Visions Inside the Administration

MR
🔴 The Hawk Marco Rubio
Secretary of State
Export controls and financial sanctions as instruments of structural rivalry.
  • Focused on America's status as Greatest Power
  • Sees tariffs and export controls as tools to contain perceived adversaries like China, Venezuela, and Cuba
  • Willing to go along with weaponization against allies, if it also targets opponents
SB
🔵 The Dealmaker Scott Bessent
Secretary of the Treasury
Tariffs and threats as opening bids for bilateral deals. Leverage is transactional, not structural.
  • Sees tariffs and chokepoints as leverage in wide-ranging negotiations with trading partners
  • Views critical mineral independence as a priority, to reduce Chinese leverage in negotiations
PN
🟢 The Protectionist Peter Navarro
Sr. Counselor for Trade & Manufacturing
Tariffs as industrial policy. The focus is reshoring manufacturing, not foreign policy.
  • "Economic security is national security"
  • Believes tariffs and export controls will rebuild U.S. manufacturing and reduce strategic risk
  • Guided by a vision of mid-20th century American prosperity supported by industry
HL
🟡 The Skeptic Howard Lutnick
Secretary of Commerce
Wary of over-reach. Excessive weaponization erodes U.S. credibility and accelerates de-risking.
  • Sometimes dismissive of economic weaponization as "mutual assured annoyance"
  • Wary of disruption to supply chains and financial markets that trade wars will cause
  • Believes economic flexibility and innovation can overcome adversaries' dominance in specific areas

Strategic liability: Allies cannot calibrate their own exposure to U.S. networks when they see no consistency. South Korea, Japan, and the Netherlands are already hedging.

Part IV
Competing Perspectives

Three schools of thought and why none is sufficient alone.

There is no consensus among strategists on how the U.S. should manage its economic toolkit. The debate maps onto three coherent but incomplete positions. Select each to explore the logic, risks, and limits:

🔴 Escalate: Press the Advantage Now

The U.S. holds structural dominance in finance and frontier technology. China is closing the gap. The window for effective coercion is finite, perhaps a decade. The U.S. should expand export controls, tighten SWIFT access, extend CFIUS screening, and exploit these advantages before rivals mature their counter-leverage.

"The semiconductor lead is real but temporary. Every year of inaction is a year of convergence."

↑ Logic
Chokepoint advantages decay. Structural leverage should be deployed while it is maximal. The Russia sanctions demonstrated that the tools work at scale.
↓ Risk
Accelerates adversary de-risking. Signals to neutral countries that U.S. network participation is dangerous. Over use of SWIFT and asset freezes erodes the dollar's perceived neutrality — the foundation of U.S. financial power.

🔵 Caution: Overreach Destroys the Foundation

Economic coercion is self-limiting. Every time the U.S. weaponizes a chokepoint, it provides incentives for building alternatives. E.g. China's mBridge digital currency project and bilateral currency swaps. The U.S. is accelerating the erosion of its own structural advantages.

"Dollar dominance rests on the perception that dollar-denominated transactions are safe. Routine asset freezes undermine that perception."

↑ Logic
Credibility and predictability are what make U.S. networks attractive. Voluntary alignment cannot be coerced into existence. Allies that feel bullied look for exits.
↓ Risk
Without coercive tools, U.S. leverage in competitive rivalry is limited to market incentives alone. This may be insufficient against a state-directed adversary willing to absorb economic pain.

🟢 Hybrid: Coercion With Guardrails

The U.S. should maintain aggressive coercion against adversaries while imposing guardrails on how allies are treated — transparency, advance warning, reversible sanctions, and consistency. Offense without credibility destroys the coalition; credibility without offense surrenders the field.

"The weaponization of economic statecraft is like opening Pandora’s box—once unleashed, it compels others to follow, setting off a chain reaction that is exceedingly difficult to contain."

↑ Logic
Combines the real benefits of coercive leverage against adversaries with the coalition maintenance needed to preserve the structural advantages that make coercion effective in the first place.
↓ Risk
Requires internal coherence and institutional discipline that the current administration has not demonstrated. Without consistent doctrine, "hybrid" defaults to ad hoc escalation in practice.

Part V
Policy Recommendation

Disciplined, Transitional Weaponization built on three pillars.

01 — The Strategic Context

The Case for a Coherent Grand Strategy

The United States has accumulated a vast toolkit of economic and security instruments over the past three decades. A coherent grand strategy that coordinates these tools toward defined ends and sustains that coordination across administrations can maximise U.S competitive advantages.

Right now, each administration inherits a set of tools, improvises new ones, and leaves behind a patchwork of authorities and commitments that the next administration must either ignore, repudiate, or absorb into its own framework. The result is strategic discontinuity — rivals can wait out any single administration; allies cannot rely on commitments that may not survive the next election.

⚠
The Ad Hoc Failure

Examples: Tariffs imposed for domestic politics undermine export controls justified on security grounds. Investment restrictions fragment allied supply chains needed for collective resilience.

⚙
Why Coherence Matters

A coherent framework allows allies to coordinate, industries to plan, Congress to legislate, and adversaries to accurately read deterrent signals. Coherence multiplies the effectiveness of any single instrument by embedding it in a recognizable strategic logic.

⏱
Durability Across Cycles

Strategies that survive election cycles are institutionalized, not merely announced. They are embedded in legislation, alliance frameworks, and bureaucratic routines that persist regardless of which party holds the executive. It is also better insulated from power abuse from the executive branch.

The recommendation that follows proposes a framework "Defense and Offense" that organizes existing and near-term instruments into a two-pillar logic, specifies which instruments belong where, and identifies the institutional and legislative architecture required to make that logic durable.

02 — The Framework

Introducing the Framework: Defense & Offense

American strategic instruments can be organised into two complementary functions:

Defense: protecting critical infrastructure, supply chains, and technological advantages from foreign exploitation.

Offense: actively shaping the international environment to expand American influence, lock in allied cooperation, and constrain rivals' strategic options.

The pairing matters because Defense alone is brittle: it can slow adversarial gains but cannot produce American advantages. Offense alone is wasteful: investments made without protecting the underlying technological base are easily appropriated. They are mutually reinforcing.

04 — Implementation

Allies, Private Sector, and Institutions

The Defense-Offense framework cannot be implemented by the U.S. government alone. It requires alliances that turns bilateral relationships into industrial coalition; a private-sector collaboration, and an institutional architecture that gives the framework statutory permanence while constraining executive overreach.

A — Allied Industrial Base: The Force Multiplier

No single country can match China's manufacturing scale, supply chain integration, or state-directed capital deployment. The U.S. competitive advantage lies in its network. Allied industrial capacity, when coordinated, creates a combined base that rivals China across semiconductors, critical minerals, advanced materials, and defense platforms.

Concretely: Japan and South Korea anchor the semiconductor ecosystem — TSMC's Arizona fabs, Samsung's Texas expansion, and Tokyo Electron's equipment supply are only viable within a U.S.-allied framework. The EU's battery gigafactory buildout reduces Western dependence on Chinese cell production. Australia's critical minerals extraction, processed through allied refining capacity, breaks China's rare-earth value chain monopoly. Co-investment serves both private commercial interest and national interests.

Future Implication: China is transitioning to smart manufacturing. Allied industrial bases along with U.S. technology are crucial to compete in the next era of industrial competition.

B — Private Sector: From Compliance Target to Strategic Partner

Economic statecraft only works if companies comply, not just with the letter of policies, but with their intent. The government cannot anticipate every workaround. After the West severed nearly every economic interaction with Russia in 2022, Russian imports from China surged 64 percent since 2021 as companies found alternative routes. Export controls and outbound investment restrictions will work only for a limited time without continual updating as countries and industries adapt.

The traditional government-business relationship is not adequate for an era in which companies must make long-term investment decisions in real time, often without clear guidance on where policy is heading. We propose the following four measures for improvements.

Commerce Consultation Office ▼

A dedicated channel at Commerce where companies can have open dialogue with policymakers — with assurance that raising an issue will not trigger regulatory scrutiny. Destigmatizes early consultation. Modeled on the Cyber Safety Review Board (DHS/CISA), which brings together public and private cybersecurity experts for structured dialogue without enforcement penalty.

Policy Intent Disclosure Requirement ▼

Every new economic security measure must include a published statement of intent and objective — specifying the targeted behavior, the sector scope, and the success criteria. This is not bureaucratic overhead; it is the precondition for companies following the spirit of the rule. The PSIRS framework (below) codifies this into statute.

Supply Chain Annual Stress Tests ▼

The Commerce Department's Supply Chain Center should conduct annual stress tests of critical supply chains — modelling how they would weather geopolitical disruptions — and share the results with industry partners. Requires increased funding; the BIS budget has been flat for a decade despite a dramatic expansion of its mandate.

Economic Intelligence Capacity ▼

Washington must invest in gathering economic intelligence — understanding China's domestic development of critical technologies and how Beijing is exploiting regulatory loopholes. Better intelligence reduces surprise, enables anticipatory policy updates, and allows companies to receive earlier guidance on emerging risk areas before restrictions are announced.

C — Institutional Architecture: Durability and Constraint

The framework requires a new statutory body to coordinate instruments across agencies and across administrations. But before making the case, it is worth asking: don't we already have bodies that do this?

Institutional & Legislative Architecture
Legislative Authority
Permanent interagency body
established by statute — not executive order — to coordinate Defense and Offense instruments across agencies. Fills the gap left by the NEC (executive order only, no cross-pillar mandate) and the NSC PCCs (working groups, not standing authority). Reports independently to both NSC and Congress; removes single-agency veto points; survives administration transitions by virtue of its legislative foundation. Includes mandatory quarterly strategic reviews and ally consultation protocols.
Trade Leverage
Section 301 / 232
Retains executive flexibility for rapid response to unfair trade practices and national security threats. Codified procedural requirements prevent purely political deployment; sunset provisions force periodic justification.
Coordinated through the interagency body
Industrial Policy Authority
CHIPS-Style Authorities
Sector-specific programs: semiconductor fabs, battery supply chains, critical minerals processing, advanced materials. Includes guardrails against subsidy competition with Tier 1/2 allies; requires allied co-investment for major grants. Private-sector applicants must demonstrate compliance with PSIRS intent-disclosure requirements.
Offensive Finance
DFC (Expanded)
Development Finance Corporation with expanded mandate and higher lending cap. Explicit authority to coordinate with Tier 2/3 ally development banks. Primary instrument for BRI competition in strategic corridors and critical mineral regions. Projects must demonstrate both commercial viability and strategic benefit — serving private-sector returns and national security simultaneously.
Gated by PSIRS before execution
Executive Constraint & Transparency Gate
PSIRS — Private Sector Intervention Review Standard
Statutory gate applied to any government intervention in a competitive private market above a defined threshold. Modeled on NEPA's environmental review logic applied to industrial policy. Every qualifying action must satisfy three codified criteria before execution: narrow scope (limited to the specific market failure or security gap identified, with written justification); transparent process (public intent statement, competitive-market impact assessment); and clear objectives (measurable outcomes with a mandatory sunset clock and performance review). Congressional notification required within 30 days; judicial review available to affected private parties. Prevents the interagency body itself from becoming a vehicle for unchecked executive discretion as the constraint applies to the coordinating body as much as to individual agencies.

Outcome
Integrated Strategic Framework
The interagency body coordinates all five authorities into coherent operational strategy. Allies are co-investors and co-designers, not just recipients of U.S. policy. The private sector operates under a clear, published intent framework — following the spirit, not just the letter, of policy. PSIRS ensures every private-sector intervention clears the scope, transparency, and objectives test before deployment. 05 — Conclusion

The Political Foundation and Honest Caveats

Domestic Distribution as Political Foundation

No grand strategy survives without a domestic political coalition to sustain it. Foreign economic policy should generate distributional benefits that build and sustain that coalition.

CHIPS-style programs do not merely produce semiconductors. They produce manufacturing jobs in competitive congressional districts, union labor agreements that make organized labor a stakeholder in the strategy, and regional economic development that gives local political elites an interest in the framework's continuation.

The protection of critical infrastructure and supply chains is politically legible across the spectrum: it is about protecting American jobs and national security, not abstract geopolitical competition.

⚠ Honest Caveat — Polarization Risk ▼

The framework assumes a baseline of legislative functionality that the current polarization environment does not guarantee. the new agency requires authorizing legislation; expanded DFC capacity requires appropriations; CHIPS-style programs require sustained multi-year funding commitments that are vulnerable to budget negotiations.

The honest assessment is that grand strategy coherence is easier to describe than to institutionalize in a divided government context. The recommendation is nonetheless that the attempt must be made — because the alternative, continued ad hoc improvisation, is demonstrably inferior even when it is more politically convenient. The costs of incoherence compound over time.

⚠ Honest Caveat — The Limits of State Capitalism ▼

The Offense pillar's industrial policy instruments carry a structural risk: overuse. Government intervention works best when it is narrow in scope, transparent in process, and clear in its objectives. Where competitive market forces exist, attempts to set prices, designate national champions, or acquire government stakes in firms tend to inflate costs, weaken competitive pressure, and crowd out other contenders.

The rare-earths case represents one of the narrow exceptions where direct government championing of individual firms is justified: China's complete cornering of a small, strategically critical market with no competitive forces at work. That logic does not extend to the semiconductor industry, a nearly $1 trillion competitive market. The goal of the Offense pillar is to reinforce the dynamism of open markets — not to replace it with the discretionary whims of politicians.

★
Core Recommendation

Adopt the Defense-Offense framework as the organizing logic for economic security policy. Establish the new agency by statute. Differentiate the alliance tier model in operational agreements. Fund the Offense pillar's distributional mandate as political infrastructure, not as a concession.

📈
Metrics for Success

Reduction in single-point supply chain vulnerabilities; increase in Tier 1/2 ally export control harmonization; measurable BRI displacement in targeted strategic corridors; bipartisan legislative support indicating distributional mandate is working.

⏱
Time Horizon

The framework is designed for a 10–15 year horizon. Near-term goals in 2–3 years: new agency establishment, updated allies agreements to provide political momentum for longer-term institutionalization.
